What Are the UN Sustainable Development Goals?

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), also known as the Global Goals, were adopted by the United Nations in 2015 as a universal call to action. These 17 interconnected goals aim to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure that by 2030 all people enjoy peace and prosperity. The SDGs recognize that action in one area affects outcomes in others, and that development must balance social, economic, and environmental sustainability. They represent a bold commitment to create a better world for current and future generations.

Understanding SDG Goals 1-5

1

No Poverty

SDG 1 aims to end poverty in all its forms everywhere by 2030. Despite significant progress—with extreme poverty rates dropping from 36% in 1990 to 10% in 2015—approximately 736 million people still live on less than $1.90 per day. Poverty is multidimensional, affecting access to food, clean water, education, and healthcare. Women are disproportionately affected due to less access to paid work, education, and property ownership. Achieving this goal requires targeting the most vulnerable populations, increasing access to basic resources and services, and building resilience against climate change and conflict.

2

Zero Hunger

SDG 2 seeks to end hunger, achieve food security, improve nutrition, and promote sustainable agriculture. The World Health Organization estimates that 12.9% of people in developing countries are undernourished, with millions of children suffering from stunting due to malnutrition. Food insecurity is exacerbated by climate change, conflict, and economic instability. This goal emphasizes not just the availability of food, but also access to nutritious, sufficient, and safe food year-round. Sustainable agricultural practices are essential to increase productivity while protecting ecosystems and biodiversity for future generations.

3

Good Health and Well-Being

SDG 3 aims to ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages. This comprehensive goal addresses maternal and child mortality, communicable diseases like HIV/AIDS and tuberculosis, non-communicable diseases, mental health, substance abuse, road traffic injuries, and access to quality healthcare services. It also emphasizes universal health coverage, ensuring that everyone can access essential health services without financial hardship. Progress requires strengthening health systems, training healthcare workers, investing in research and development, and addressing social determinants of health such as poverty and education that significantly impact health outcomes.

Quality Education

SDG 4 strives to ensure inclusive and equitable quality education and promote lifelong learning opportunities for all. Education is one of the most powerful tools for sustainable development, poverty reduction, and gender equality. While significant progress has been made—with primary school enrollment in developing regions reaching 91% in 2015—millions of children, especially girls and those in conflict zones, remain out of school. Large disparities exist based on wealth, location (rural vs. urban), and gender. This goal emphasizes not just access to education, but also quality, relevance, and outcomes, ensuring that all children complete free primary and secondary education and have access to affordable vocational and tertiary education.

Gender inequality persists worldwide, manifesting in discrimination, violence, unequal access to education and employment, underrepresentation in leadership positions, and harmful practices like child marriage and female genital mutilation. SDG Target 5.6: Universal Access to Menstrual Health and Hygiene

Target 5.6 specifically aims to "ensure universal access to sexual and reproductive health and reproductive rights." Menstrual health and hygiene (MHH) is a critical component of this target, affecting 1.8 billion people worldwide who menstruate every month.

Understanding the Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a natural, healthy biological process that typically occurs monthly in individuals with a uterus during their reproductive years, usually from ages 12-13 (menarche) to around 45-55 (menopause). The cycle averages 28 days but can range from 21-35 days. It involves hormonal changes that prepare the body for potential pregnancy. The cycle has four phases: the menstrual phase (days 1-5), during which the uterine lining sheds and exits through the vagina as menstrual blood; the follicular phase, when follicles in the ovaries mature; ovulation (around day 14), when an egg is released; and the luteal phase, when the uterine lining thickens. If pregnancy doesn't occur, the cycle repeats. Understanding this natural process is essential for health literacy and removing stigma.

Menstrual Hygiene Management: Best Practices

Proper menstrual hygiene management (MHM) is essential for health, dignity, and well-being. According to the WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme, MHM means using clean menstrual materials to absorb or collect blood, changed in privacy as often as necessary (typically every 4-8 hours), with soap and water for washing the body, and safe disposal facilities. Key practices include:

  • Use clean, safe products: Options include disposable pads, tampons, menstrual cups, or clean reusable cloth pads. Products should be changed regularly to prevent infections.
  • Maintain personal hygiene: Wash hands before and after changing menstrual products. Clean the genital area with water and mild soap.
  • Proper disposal: Wrap used products in paper or biodegradable bags before disposal. Never flush pads or tampons. Reusable products should be washed with soap and water and dried completely in sunlight.
  • Access to WASH facilities: Private, clean toilets with water, soap, and disposal bins are essential, especially in schools and workplaces.
  • Track your cycle: Understanding your menstrual pattern helps manage it better and identify potential health issues.
  • Seek medical care when needed: Consult healthcare providers for severe pain, irregular cycles, excessive bleeding, or signs of infection.

Breaking the Silence: Addressing Social Stigma

Despite menstruation being a normal biological process, cultural taboos and discriminatory social norms continue to surround it in many societies. Menstruation is often seen as impure or shameful, leading to practices that restrict menstruators from participating in daily activities, religious ceremonies, cooking, or even attending school. This stigma creates a culture of silence where girls and women lack accurate information about menstruation before menarche, leading to fear, confusion, and unhygienic practices. The lack of open discussion perpetuates misconceptions and negative attitudes, motivating shaming, bullying, and sometimes gender-based violence.

Breaking this stigma requires comprehensive menstrual health education for all genders—not just those who menstruate—starting before menarche (around age 9). Education should cover the biology of menstruation, proper hygiene practices, and the importance of treating menstruation as normal and healthy. Creating supportive environments where menstruation can be discussed openly without embarrassment is crucial. Media campaigns, community engagement, and including menstrual health in school curricula all contribute to changing attitudes and normalizing menstruation.

Creating Supportive Environments: School and College Measures

Educational institutions play a critical role in supporting menstrual health and ensuring that menstruation doesn't become a barrier to education. Research shows that millions of girls miss school during their periods due to inadequate facilities, lack of products, or stigma, leading to learning gaps and sometimes dropout. Schools and colleges should implement:

  • Adequate WASH infrastructure: Clean, private, gender-segregated toilets with locks, running water, soap, and covered disposal bins in every school.
  • Access to menstrual products: Free or affordable pads, tampons, or reusable products should be available through dispensers or health rooms, ensuring no student misses class due to lack of products.
  • Comprehensive menstrual health education: Include menstrual health in health and biology curricula starting from grade 4-5, before most students reach menarche. Provide accurate, age-appropriate information to all students regardless of gender.
  • Trained staff and supportive policies: Teachers and health staff should be trained to support students experiencing menstruation, including providing pain relief and allowing bathroom breaks. Policies should not penalize absence due to severe menstrual symptoms.
  • Create awareness and reduce stigma: Organize workshops, awareness campaigns, and peer education programs that normalize menstruation and combat harmful myths.
  • Pain management support: Provide access to pain relievers and rest areas for students experiencing severe menstrual cramps (dysmenorrhea).

These measures ensure that menstruation doesn't become a barrier to education and helps create a supportive environment where students can learn and thrive during all days of the month.

Resources and Support Networks

Addressing menstrual health requires collaboration across multiple sectors including health, education, water and sanitation, and social protection. Various organizations and resources provide support:

  • Healthcare services: Access to gynecologists, reproductive health clinics, and school health services for counseling, treatment of menstrual disorders, and pain management.
  • NGOs and community organizations: Groups like the Global Menstrual Collective, WASH United, and Days for Girls work globally to improve menstrual health through education, product distribution, and advocacy.
  • Government programs: Many countries now have national menstrual health strategies, subsidized or free menstrual products in schools, and removal of taxes on menstrual products.
  • Helplines and counseling: Confidential helplines and online resources provide information and support for menstrual health concerns.
  • Peer support groups: School-based or community menstrual health clubs create safe spaces for discussion and support.
  • Research and innovation: Ongoing research into affordable, sustainable menstrual products and improved understanding of menstrual health conditions benefits all menstruators.

Remember: Menstrual health is a health and human rights issue. Everyone deserves access to information, products, facilities, and supportive environments to manage menstruation with dignity and without shame.
